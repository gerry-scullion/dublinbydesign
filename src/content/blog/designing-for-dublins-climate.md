Dublin's climate is often seen as a constraint. Designers complain about the grey skies, the persistent drizzle, the unpredictable winds. But the best design doesn't fight the climate — it works with it.

## Learning from the North

Scandinavian cities have long understood this. In Copenhagen and Helsinki, public spaces are designed to be usable year-round. Covered walkways, heated benches, and wind-sheltered seating areas make outdoor life possible even in December.

## What Dublin Gets Right

Dublin already has some excellent examples of climate-responsive design:

- **The Iveagh Gardens** use mature tree canopy and sheltered walls to create a microclimate that feels several degrees warmer than the surrounding streets
- **Meeting House Square** in Temple Bar has a retractable canopy that responds to weather conditions
- **The Docklands boardwalks** are oriented to catch afternoon sun and shelter from prevailing winds

## Principles for Climate Design

1. **Shelter without enclosure** — Covered areas that still feel outdoor, using canopies, colonnades, and deep overhangs
2. **Wind management** — Using planting and built form to reduce wind speed at ground level without creating dead zones
3. **Daylight optimisation** — Maximising natural light through material choices, building orientation, and reflective surfaces
4. **Rain as a feature** — Rain chains, water features, and permeable surfaces that celebrate water rather than hiding it

## The Opportunity

As Dublin continues to grow and densify, climate-responsive design isn't a luxury — it's a necessity. Every new public space, every streetscape improvement, every building facade is an opportunity to make the city more comfortable, more liveable, and more distinctly Dublin.
